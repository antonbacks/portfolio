Case Study

Voice & Tone Guidelines for FinTech

UX Writing Content Strategy

Hero image placeholder — 16:9

Role
Lead UX Writer
Timeline
12 weeks
Team
1 writer, 2 designers, 1 brand strategist
Tools
Notion, Figma, Google Docs

The Challenge

The company offered personal finance tools — budgeting, investing, and lending — but the product voice was all over the place. The investment section read like a legal disclaimer, the budgeting tool was overly casual, and error messages ranged from robotic to alarming.

Users trusted the product's features but described it as "confusing" and "cold" in satisfaction surveys. For a financial product, trust depends on clarity, and clarity depends on consistent, human language.

Content Audit

I cataloged over 300 strings across the product — buttons, headings, tooltips, error states, empty states, confirmation dialogs, and email notifications. I tagged each for tone (formal/informal), clarity (clear/jargon-heavy), and consistency (matches brand/doesn't match).

The audit revealed that 40% of user-facing text contained financial jargon without explanation, and the tone shifted dramatically between product areas. There was no shared vocabulary — the same concept was called "transfer," "move," and "send" in different places.

Content audit spreadsheet & tone mapping — placeholder

Defining the Voice

Through workshops with stakeholders, I facilitated exercises to define the brand's voice attributes. We landed on four principles: Clear (no jargon without explanation), Confident (reassuring without overpromising), Warm (human, not robotic), and Respectful (never condescending about money).

For each principle, I created a spectrum showing how tone should shift based on context. A celebratory moment (reaching a savings goal) could be warmer and more enthusiastic, while an error with a failed transaction should be calm, clear, and action-oriented.

Voice principles & tone spectrum — placeholder

The Guidelines

The final deliverable was a living Notion document organized into practical sections: Voice principles with examples, a tone adaptation framework for different scenarios, a word list (preferred terms vs. avoided terms), component-specific writing patterns, and a "before/after" gallery of rewritten UI text.

I made the guidelines actionable rather than abstract. Instead of "be friendly," the guide showed: Before: "Transaction failed. Error code 4012." After: "This payment didn't go through. Your card issuer declined the transaction — you might want to try a different card or contact your bank."

Implementation

I partnered with the design team to integrate the guidelines into Figma as a text styles library with usage notes. I also rewrote the 50 highest-traffic strings myself as reference implementations, showing the team what "good" looked like in practice.

To support adoption, I held weekly "copy reviews" where designers and PMs could bring strings for feedback. These became the most popular recurring meeting on the team calendar.

Results

28% improvement in comprehension scores for key flows (measured via task-based testing). "Trustworthy" moved from 5th to 1st in brand attribute surveys. 45% reduction in support tickets categorized as "confusion." Copy review adoption — 85% of new features went through copy review within 3 months.

The product's app store rating improved from 3.8 to 4.4, with multiple reviews specifically mentioning how "easy to understand" the app had become.

Reflection

This project showed me that content strategy isn't just about writing better words — it's about building systems that help everyone write better words. The guidelines were only valuable because they were practical, accessible, and maintained. Abstract voice documents gather dust; actionable word lists and before/after examples get used every day.
