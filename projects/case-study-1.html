Case Study

Redesigning the Checkout Experience

UX Design UX Writing Research

Hero image placeholder — 16:9

Role
Lead UX Designer
Timeline
8 weeks
Team
2 designers, 3 engineers, 1 PM
Tools
Figma, Maze, Hotjar

The Challenge

The existing checkout was a 5-step process with high drop-off rates. Analytics showed 67% of users abandoned their cart, with the biggest losses occurring between the shipping and payment steps. Customer support tickets frequently mentioned confusion around delivery options and unexpected costs.

We needed to simplify the flow without removing essential information, while also improving the clarity of every label, error message, and call-to-action along the way.

Discovery & Research

I started with a heuristic evaluation of the existing checkout, documenting 23 usability issues ranging from unclear form labels to missing progress indicators. I then conducted 8 moderated usability sessions, watching real users struggle through the flow.

Key findings from research:

Users were surprised by shipping costs appearing late in the flow. The payment form asked for information in an unintuitive order. Error messages were vague ("Invalid input") rather than actionable. The "Continue" button didn't clearly indicate what came next.

Research findings & affinity map — placeholder

Design Approach

Rather than incremental fixes, I proposed consolidating the five steps into a single scrollable page with clear visual sections. This eliminated the anxiety of an unknown number of remaining steps and let users see the full picture before committing.

For every form field, I rewrote the labels and helper text with the principle: "Tell users what they need to know, when they need to know it." Error messages became specific and helpful — "Please enter the 3 digits on the back of your card" instead of "Invalid CVV."

Before & after checkout flow comparison — placeholder

Prototyping & Testing

I created a high-fidelity prototype in Figma and ran two rounds of usability testing with 12 participants total. The first round surfaced issues with the shipping cost summary — users still felt uncertain. I iterated on a live cost breakdown that updated as they typed their address.

The second round validated the design. Task completion time dropped from an average of 4 minutes 20 seconds to 2 minutes 10 seconds, and participants rated the experience 4.6/5 for clarity.

Results

After launch, we measured the impact over 6 weeks against the previous quarter's baseline:

23% reduction in cart abandonment rate. 35% fewer checkout-related support tickets. 48 seconds faster average completion time. 4.7/5 post-purchase satisfaction rating (up from 3.2).

The most rewarding feedback came from the support team, who said customers had stopped asking "Where did my discount code go?" — because the new design kept it visible throughout.

Reflection

This project reinforced my belief that the best UX improvements often come from writing, not just layout changes. Clearer labels, better error messages, and honest pricing reduced friction more than any visual redesign could alone. It also taught me the value of testing early — the first round of feedback fundamentally changed my approach to the shipping section.
