Case Study

Building a Design System from Scratch

UX Design Design Systems

Hero image placeholder — 16:9

Role
Design System Lead
Timeline
6 months
Team
3 designers, 4 engineers
Tools
Figma, Storybook, Notion

The Challenge

The company had grown from one product to four in two years, but the design language hadn't kept up. Each team had developed its own patterns — four different button styles, three modal implementations, and inconsistent spacing that made the products feel disconnected.

Beyond visual inconsistency, this was a velocity problem. Designers were rebuilding the same components for each project, and engineers were implementing variations they shouldn't have needed to build.

Audit & Inventory

I began with a comprehensive interface inventory, cataloging every component, color, and type style across all four products. The results were stark: 14 shades of blue, 8 button variants, and 6 different approaches to form validation.

I also interviewed designers and engineers from each team to understand their pain points and workflows. The biggest frustration was universal: "I know this component exists somewhere, but I can't find it, so I build my own."

Component audit & inventory results — placeholder

Foundation First

Rather than jumping straight to components, I focused on foundations: a unified color palette derived from the brand, a type scale with clear hierarchy, a spacing system based on an 8px grid, and elevation/shadow tokens.

I documented the reasoning behind every decision in Notion, so that future designers could understand not just what the tokens were, but why. This philosophy of "document the why" became a core principle of the system.

Design tokens & foundation documentation — placeholder

Component Architecture

I designed components with composability in mind. Each component had clearly defined states (default, hover, active, disabled, error), responsive behavior, and accessibility requirements baked in from the start.

The Figma library used auto-layout extensively and component properties for variant switching, making it fast for designers to assemble screens without detaching. Engineers built corresponding React components in Storybook with matching prop APIs.

Component architecture & Figma structure — placeholder

Adoption & Governance

A design system only works if people use it. I ran onboarding workshops for each team, created a contribution model for new components, and set up a Slack channel for questions and requests. Monthly "design system reviews" kept alignment as products evolved.

The contribution model was intentionally lightweight: propose in Slack, design review with two approvals, build and document. Low friction meant high participation.

Results

40% faster design-to-development handoff. 60% reduction in "one-off" component requests. 92% adoption across product teams within 4 months. 100% of new features built with system components after month 5.

The biggest win wasn't the numbers — it was hearing a new hire say "Everything just makes sense, it's like one product" during their first week.

Reflection

The hardest part of building a design system isn't the design — it's the people. Getting four teams to agree on a single button style required diplomacy, empathy, and a lot of listening. I learned that governance matters more than aesthetics, and that documentation is a product in itself.
