Case Study

Equal Pay Dashboard for EU Compliance

UX Design UX Writing Data Visualization

Team

The Challenge

EU Pay Transparency Directive requires companies to analyze and report pay equity data. The client — a mid-size Swedish tech company — had raw payroll data but no way to visualize it, spot patterns, or generate compliance reports. HR was spending 3 days per quarter manually compiling spreadsheets that nobody could interpret at a glance.

They needed a dashboard that made pay equity data not just accessible, but actionable.

Research & Discovery

I interviewed 6 HR leaders and 3 compliance officers across Nordic companies to understand their workflows and pain points. I also conducted competitive analysis of existing HR analytics tools.

The key insight: users needed two distinct modes — an exploration mode for day-to-day monitoring ("Is there a gap in Engineering?") and a reporting mode for quarterly compliance submissions. This shaped the dual-tab architecture: Overview for exploration, Trends for reporting.

Information Architecture

The dashboard architecture prioritized progressive disclosure. The Overview tab surfaces the four most critical KPIs immediately — gender distribution, average salary, salary distribution, and overall pay gap — before letting users drill into charts and individual employee data.

The Trends tab serves the reporting use case with longitudinal data and department-level breakdowns. I tested the hierarchy with card sorting exercises with 8 HR professionals, which validated the KPI prioritization order.

UX Writing & Microcopy

This project was as much a writing challenge as a design one. Key decisions: I chose "Pay gap" over "Wage gap" after testing showed 73% of users associated "pay gap" with total compensation while "wage gap" implied hourly rates only.

The insight panel uses a conversational voice ("Here's what we're seeing") to make data feel approachable rather than clinical. Filter labels use plain language ("Department" not "Organizational unit") and the compliance badge ("Compliant" / "Action needed") was written to be actionable without being alarming.

Every tooltip, empty state, and chart label was written to reduce cognitive load for non-technical HR users.

Visual Design & Data Visualization

I chose a purple/indigo palette to differentiate from the typical blue corporate dashboard aesthetic while maintaining professionalism. The color system uses purple (#6C5CE7) for women's data and teal-green (#00B894) for men's — deliberately avoiding pink/blue gender stereotypes.

Chart design principles: minimal gridlines, generous whitespace, direct labels over legends where possible, and consistent number formatting (SEK currency, one decimal for percentages). I tested color contrast ratios for WCAG AA compliance and verified chart readability with color vision deficiency simulations.

Interactive Prototype

I built a fully interactive prototype to demonstrate the dashboard's functionality beyond static mockups. The prototype includes live filtering, sortable tables, animated charts, and tab navigation — giving stakeholders a realistic feel for the final product.

Results

Reduced quarterly reporting time from 3 days to 20 minutes. The dashboard identified 3 previously hidden department-level pay gaps that manual spreadsheets had missed.

The company achieved full EU Pay Transparency Directive compliance 3 months ahead of schedule. User satisfaction score of 4.8/5 from HR team members. The design was later adopted as a template for the company's other compliance dashboards.

Reflection

This project crystallized something I'd been feeling for a while: data visualization is fundamentally a UX writing challenge. The most impactful design decisions weren't about chart types or color palettes — they were about labels.

Calling something "Pay gap: 4.8%" versus "Average compensation differential: 4.8% (favorable)" completely changes how a user interprets and acts on the data. Working on this dashboard taught me that in data-heavy products, the words do more heavy lifting than the visuals. A well-labeled simple chart beats a beautiful but cryptic one every time.
