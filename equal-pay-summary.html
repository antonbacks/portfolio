The Challenge

The EU Pay Transparency Directive requires companies to analyze and report pay equity data. The client — a mid-size Swedish tech company — had raw payroll data but no way to visualize it, spot patterns, or generate compliance reports. HR spent 3 days per quarter manually compiling spreadsheets. They needed a dashboard that made pay equity data accessible and actionable.

Research & Discovery

Interviewed 6 HR leaders and 3 compliance officers across Nordic companies. Conducted competitive analysis of existing HR analytics tools.

Key insight: Users need two distinct modes — exploration for day-to-day monitoring and reporting for quarterly compliance. This shaped the dual-tab architecture (Overview + Trends).

Information Architecture

Progressive disclosure approach: Overview tab surfaces 4 critical KPIs immediately (gender distribution, average salary, salary distribution, pay gap), then lets users drill into charts and individual data. Trends tab serves reporting with longitudinal data and department breakdowns.

UX Writing Decisions

  • "Pay gap" over "Wage gap" — 73% of users associated "pay gap" with total compensation
  • Insight panel uses conversational voice: "Here's what we're seeing"
  • Plain language labels: "Department" not "Organizational unit"
  • Compliance badge: "Compliant" / "Action needed" — actionable without being alarming
  • Every tooltip, empty state, and chart label written to reduce cognitive load
